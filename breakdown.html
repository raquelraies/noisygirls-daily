Break it down
Paste an article headline or the line that caught you. Work through the five steps. Get your talking points — without a script.
1
The real claim
What it's actually saying underneath the headline
Provocation
▼
Strip the headline. What is this article actually arguing? Not a summary — the uncomfortable claim underneath it. One sentence.
Example
"This study is saying women who are ambitious are less likeable — which means the system is still designed for us to fail quietly."
2
Your gut reaction
Feel it before you think it
Instinct
▼
Before you analyse it — what did you feel when you read it? Annoyed? Validated? Surprised? Smug? That feeling IS the content. Start there.
Example
"My first reaction was — of course, we already knew this. But then I got angry that we still have to keep talking about it in 2026."
3
The personal pull
Make it real with one specific moment
Lived experience
▼
Does this connect to something you've seen, lived, or noticed? One specific moment — not a lecture. The more specific, the better. Vague = boring. Specific = compelling.
Example
"I had a client last year who literally apologised for asking for a pay rise. A man in the same role never would have. Never."
4
The noisy question
The question that starts the real conversation
The debate
▼
What would you actually want to argue about? Not "what do you think?" — something with an edge. Something Siobhan could genuinely push back on. Make it uncomfortable.
Example
"Are women complicit in keeping this going? Because I think some of us absolutely are — and nobody wants to say it out loud."
5
The wildcard
The take that complicates everything
The twist
▼
The unexpected angle. The thing you almost don't want to admit. What complicates everything you just said? This is what makes the conversation unpredictable.
Example
"But also — is likeability even worth having? Maybe we've been optimising for the wrong metric entirely and the whole frame is broken."
